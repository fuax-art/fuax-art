The Aesthetics of Decay

      There’s a quiet poetry in the way time unravels the things we build.
Old factories with broken windows and rusted beams slowly surrender to vines and wind—forgotten spaces becoming canvases for light, silence, and memory. It’s strange, isn’t it? How decay, something we’re taught to avoid or fix, can end up being so breathtaking. It’s not just what’s falling apart—it’s what’s being revealed.
What once stood for progress now tells a different kind of story: of persistence, of nature’s patience, of the elegance in imperfection. There’s a grace in things left untouched, undone, unfinished.
And when we create, maybe we can borrow from that. Let the mess show. Let the wear and weather shine through. Treat absence as presence—what’s missing becomes part of the meaning.
To see beauty in decay is to recognize that nothing lasts, and that’s the point. It’s the echo, the fading, the softness after the storm that stirs something true.
We aren’t just making things to last—we’re making things that feel. And that? That’s a different kind of forever. Forever Unfinished.
